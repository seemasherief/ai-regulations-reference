The four scenarios this reference is built to prevent
A customer denied service unfairly by AI
An automated triage system routes a customer away from human support. No knowledge AI made the decision. No recourse to challenge it. At scale, this becomes systemic discrimination.
A biased AI decision at scale
A CS AI trained on historical data inherits those biases. Non-native speakers, older customers, accessibility needs - systematically underserved. Invisible until measured. Then catastrophic.
A fine the company didn't see coming
A third-party CS AI tool transfers customer data across regions without adequate legal basis. A supervisory authority investigation follows. The fine arrives before the compliance team knew there was a problem.
A system change with no governance trail
A CS AI's model is updated without notifying the deploying organisation. They cannot maintain an accurate system record. When a regulator asks for documentation, there is none.

Implementation timeline
!

AI literacy obligations active. Prohibited AI practices enforceable. Organisations must have reviewed their AI systems against the prohibited practices list.
!

General purpose AI model providers face full transparency and copyright obligations. Governance rules for high-level AI now enforceable.

Extended transition period ends for high-risk AI systems embedded in regulated products. Full Annex III obligations enforceable across all categories.
Why this reference was built
The methodology, the motivation, and what drove the article selection
The starting point
"My starting point was not a regulatory framework. It was a concern. AI without guardrails is not just ineffective, it is dangerous."
I had been reading about AI systems deployed without adequate controls. When I started thinking about what this means for customer service specifically, systems making decisions about real customer at scale in real time, the concern became concrete. I started with the EU AI Act because it felt like the most structured answer to a question I was already asking: what does responsible AI deployment actually require, and who is accountable when it goes wrong?
How articles were selected
Each article was tested against one question: would this have prevented what happened to Tay? Would this protect a customer being denied service unfairly by an AI today?
Articles were excluded when they addressed regulatory infrastructure irrelevant to a deploying organisation, applied exclusively to high-risk systems that most CS AI does not currently meet or duplicated obligations already captured more specifically elsewhere.
What emerged across all four frameworks is that the same four themes appear repeatedly. Just formalised differently: transparency, fairness, human oversight, and accountability.
What this reference does not cover — and why
excluded
EU AI Act - High-risk obligations (Articles 16–25)
Applies only to high-risk AI systems as defined in Annex III. Most CS AI currently falls below this threshold. If a CS AI system makes consequential decisions about access to essential services, these articles become applicable and should be assessed separately.
excluded
GDPR - Data Protection Officer obligations (Articles 37–39)
DPO appointment depends on scale and nature of processing. Organisations processing customer data at significant scale should assess DPO requirements as a separate workstream.
excluded
National implementing legislation
Spain has introduced AI Act fines of up to 7% of turnover. Several EU member states have introduced additional provisions beyond the Act. These require jurisdiction-specific assessment.
scoped
SB 942 - text-only CS AI is currently outside scope
SB 942 applies to GenAI producing image, video, or audio. Chatbots and routing systems generating text only are outside SB 942's current scope. CCPA continues to apply regardless.
About this portfolio
My background is 12 years in customer service operations at Amazon, working across Operations, Tech, Legal and senior leadership on systems that interact with customers at scale. I always wondered about what happens when systems that touch real customers fail? The operational consequences, the human cost, the gap between what was designed and what was deployed. AI governance is the formalisation of questions I have been asking in operational contexts.
